\section{Implementation}

\subsection{Code: Pipeline Explanation}
The 3D scene reconstruction pipeline was implemented using Python, NumPy, and Open3D. Below is a detailed breakdown of the methodologies applied at each stage:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Loading \& Depth Quantization Fix:} 
    The original script was modified to preserve depth as a 16-bit unsigned integer. This preserved sub-millimeter geometric accuracy and adhered to the specification's \texttt{DEPTH\_SCALE = 1000.0}.

    \item \textbf{Task 1: Geometric Unprojection:}
    Utilized the Pinhole Camera Model. Instead of using loops, I utilized vectorized NumPy operations with \texttt{np.meshgrid} to map pixel coordinates to $(X, Y, Z)$ spatial coordinates. The Y-axis was inverted to align with Habitat's world space.

    \item \textbf{Pre-processing:}
    Applied Voxel Downsampling to reduce the point count for registration math. Surface normals were estimated using a KD-Tree search, and Fast Point Feature Histograms (FPFH) were computed to facilitate global registration.

    \item \textbf{Registration Pipeline:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Global Registration (RANSAC):} Used to find the initial coarse alignment. I utilized a high iteration count (up to 1.5M) to ensure a high probability of success given low inlier ratios ($\sim$1-3\%) caused by discrete 0.25m agent steps.
        \item \textbf{Local Registration (ICP):} Used Point-to-Plane ICP with a convergence criteria to refine the RANSAC result, locking planar surfaces together.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Post-processing (Ceiling Removal):}
    Implemented a height-based mask to remove the ceiling for visualization. This is integrated into the evaluation phase rather than the reconstruction phase to maintain the geometric integrity of the raw data.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Discussion \& Findings}
Based on the experimental results, several key insights were identified:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Iteration Bottleneck (Exp 3):} Reducing RANSAC iterations from 1.5M to 100k caused the Mean L2 Error to jump significantly (from 0.038m to 0.198m). Because the agent's step size in Habitat is 0.25m, the inlier ratio is extremely low ($\approx$1-3\%). Under these conditions, the stochastic nature of RANSAC requires a higher "search budget" to find the correct transformation; otherwise, it converges to local minima.
    \item \textbf{Voxel Trade-offs (Exp 2):} Increasing the voxel size to 0.08m achieved the fastest execution time (53s), but at a significant cost to accuracy. The coarser geometry makes it difficult for Point-to-Plane ICP to calculate accurate normals, leading to increased odometry drift.
    \item \textbf{Thresholding (Exp 4):} The "Reach Test" demonstrated that a tight RANSAC threshold (0.048m) makes the system less robust to Habitat's discrete movement. If the threshold is smaller than the noise or the actual jump between frames, RANSAC fails to identify valid correspondences, leading to higher error.
    \item \textbf{Feature Optimization (Exp 5):} Reducing the FPFH nearest neighbors to 50 maintained high accuracy (0.038m). This suggests that for indoor environments with many planar surfaces, local geometric signatures do not require high neighbor density to remain descriptive.
\end{itemize}

\section{Questions}

\subsection{What happens if you perform ICP without Global Registration (RANSAC)? Why?}
If standard ICP is performed without an initial coarse alignment from RANSAC, the algorithm will frequently fail or converge into a local minimum. 

\textbf{Why:} ICP is a local optimization algorithm. If the camera undergoes a large translation (0.25m) or rotation, the true corresponding points fall outside of the ICP search radius. Consequently, ICP will match points to the wrong neighbors (e.g., matching a chair leg to a wall), locking the map into an incorrect, disjointed pose. RANSAC is required to provide the "initial guess" that places the point clouds within the convergence basin of the true solution.

\subsection{Describe any tricks used to improve your ICP stability.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Point-to-Plane Estimation:} In geometric indoor environments, Point-to-Plane ICP allows the current frame to mathematically slide along planar surfaces (walls/floors), preventing the "stuck" behavior often seen in Point-to-Point estimation.
    \item \textbf{Voxel-Based Normal Estimation:} Using a radius-based normal estimation (2x voxel size) ensures that even with downsampled data, the underlying surface geometry is accurately represented.
    \item \textbf{Trajectory Visualization:} Representing camera poses as 3D spheres rather than just lines allowed for easier identification of "jumps" or "teleportation" errors during the tuning process.
\end{itemize}
